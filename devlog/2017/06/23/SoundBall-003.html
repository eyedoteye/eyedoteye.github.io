SoundBall #003: Iteration 2 Complete

Jun 23, 2017

This week I’ve finished up the second iteration of this mindful sound-piano-thing experiment. And now, for a post-mortem!

First, the technicals:
I’m not sure javascript was the right fit for me for prototyping. It’s quick to develop, and quick to share, but only for the most straightforward of ideas. There are also some latency issues, even though I’m not doing anything particularly intensive. And that’s no good! As I’m not actually concerned with the quick-sharing aspect, I’m planning on making a switch over to unity. I think both approaches have a bit of a learning curve before an acceptable prototyping speed can be achieved, but unity promises far more, in terms of testing more complex ideas.

Now, the design:
Although there is some substance in the current iteration, as a whole, the design is currently unsuccesful.

I like the exploration into heavyness, but the slipping part is unnessential to this plaything’s purpose. Originally it was there as an ‘encouragement’ for the player to drop the ball and see the frequencies collapse into a note, however in practice, this slipping is frustrating, and since the center of the ball determines the current playing frequency, it only serves to weaken the mindful connection between sound and player.

The ‘encouragement’ has also been replaced by the starting state of the ball. I imagine that the start screen for this game will have the ball hovering, and on start the ball will fall and collapse down the first note. This will clearly demonstrate what the main functionality of this plaything is.

Also, the intuition of the exponentially increasing frequency of each ascending note is largely lost. The game is currently too much of a move this ball above the line and drop kind of deal. It lacks an emphasis in the exploration needed to imbue a mindful play.

Graphically, putting aside roughness due to being a prototype, the gradients need to be expanded into something more visually meaningful. Currrently, the compressed compression that they hold is visually lost. (Also, they are somewhat incorrect, but that’s not as important as it can be patched up later.)

It is also non obvious that the piano keys are pressable. It is most certainly worth the effort to improve this. Changing the keys to be more piano-like could solve this problem, as well as improve the connection between frequency and note for the player.

2017-06-27: I’ve made some usability updates based on initial feedback. The rising pitch sound was completely inaudible so I’ve shifted the octave up two and increased the sound volume. Also, I realized for some reason I was starting the piano roll on a C note. The new piano roll starts on A4.
Previously the ball was unable to be picked up by the parts of it that were underneath the first note line. This was a leftover from an earlier build were that part of the ball was drawn over. This has been fixed.
I’ve even gone a step further and allowed the ball to take selection priority so it can be picked up even if it’s underneath a piano key.

This is why playtesting is super important!

These changes are more of a band-aid than a proper fix. Pure higher frequencies tend to evoke an unpleasantness. In the future, I think pitch-shifting a flavored tone would be preferable. (Maybe from a sound bowl?)

I think I will spend more time exploring heavyness as a separate project. It’s pretty interesting! But also, I will now spend time re-designing a bunch of things and re-approaching this in Unity! It should serve as a good project for learning the ropes.
